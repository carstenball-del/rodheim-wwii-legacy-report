## Executive Summary

1st Lt. Seymour Rodney Rodheim served as a bombardier in the United States Army Air Forces during World War II. Assigned to the 366th Bomb Squadron, 305th Bombardment Group (Heavy) at RAF Chelveston, he flew B-17G Flying Fortresses. On his mission of 11 May 1944 targeting industrial and transportation infrastructure in Saarbrücken, Germany, his aircraft (42-39966 "Swinging Door" aka "V-For Victory") was struck by flak. Two crew members, including Rodheim and 2/Lt Norbert J. Stefaniak, bailed out over German territory. The remainder of the crew successfully returned the damaged bomber to England, crash-landing near Canterbury.

Rodheim evaded capture for 13 days with assistance from local civilians, including nuns at a monastery, coming within a few miles of the border before being apprehended by the Gestapo. He was interned as a Prisoner of War at Stalag Luft III in Sagan (now Żagan, Poland), the famous camp for Allied air officers. He endured the brutal Sagan Death March in late January/early February 1945 as Soviet forces approached, before eventual liberation and repatriation.

Post-war, Rodheim returned to civilian life. He passed away in 1966 from non-Hodgkin lymphoma at a relatively young age, survived by his daughter Debra R. Ball (who passed in December 2024) and family. His granddaughter's generation, particularly through Carsten Ball's dedicated advocacy, is now actively preserving and honoring his service through records requests, posthumous awards pursuit, and donation of the "Rodheim Collection" to the National WWII Museum in New Orleans.

## 2. Military Enlistment, Training, and Commissioning

Rodheim entered service in the U.S. Army Air Forces and was commissioned as a 2nd Lieutenant (later 1st Lieutenant), service number O-757851. As a bombardier, he would have undergone specialized training in bombing techniques, navigation aids, bombardier school (likely at one of the Army Air Forces training fields such as those in Texas or New Mexico), and crew coordination for B-17 operations.

Bombardiers were critical crew members responsible for the Norden bombsight, target identification, bomb release timing, and often defensive gunnery in the nose. Training emphasized precision, especially for the strategic bombing campaign against Nazi Germany' s industrial and military infrastructure.

By early 1944, he was ready for overseas deployment to the European Theater of Operations (ETO), assigned to the Eighth Air Force.

## 3. Assignment to the 305th Bombardment Group (Heavy) and the Aircraft

The 305th BG, nicknamed "Can Do," was one of the pioneering heavy bombardment groups of the Eighth Air Force. Activated in 1942, it flew B-17s from RAF Chelveston, England, conducting strategic bombing missions deep into occupied Europe. The group earned Distinguished Unit Citations and contributed significantly to the Combined Bomber Offensive.

Rodheim was assigned to the 366th Bomb Squadron. The specific aircraft associated with the May 11, 1944 mission was B-17G-10-VE serial number 42-39966, nicknamed "Swinging Door" and also known as "V-For Victory." It was a Lockheed/Vega-built B-17G delivered in October 1943, assigned to Chelveston on 5 January 1944. Radio call letters: KY-S.

**Aircraft History Note:** This B-17 had a relatively short but eventful combat career. On the mission of 11 May 1944, it sustained damage but returned to base area. It was later lost on 3 August 1944 during a mission to Merkville (or similar), with a different crew; all nine became POWs (MACR 7702). Carsten Ball has indicated possession of the vast majority of the MACR 7702 report.

## 4. The Fateful Mission of 11 May 1944

On 11 May 1944, the 305th BG participated in a mission targeting Saarbrücken (Saarbrucken), Germany – a key industrial city and transportation hub in the Saar region, important for coal, steel, and rail networks supporting the German war effort.

The aircraft 42-39966 was hit by anti-aircraft fire (flak) over or near the target area. In accordance with standard procedures for severely damaged bombers, two crew members bailed out over German-held territory to reduce weight and increase chances for the aircraft to limp home. The remaining crew nursed the plane back across the Channel, crash-landing near Canterbury, England.

**Crew Members Who Bailed Out:**
- 2/Lt Norbert J. Stefaniak
- 2/Lt Seymour Rodheim (Bombardier)

Both were captured and became POWs. The rest of the crew survived the return and landing.

This mission occurred during the intense pre-D-Day bombing campaign (spring 1944) aimed at softening German defenses and infrastructure for the upcoming Normandy invasion (Operation Overlord, June 6, 1944).

**MACR 4874:** This Missing Air Crew Report documents the incident for the two who bailed out. Full details are held in NARA microfilm records (M1380 series). Carsten Ball and family have been actively requesting and reviewing related records.

## 5. Evasion: 13 Days Behind Enemy Lines

Following bailout, Lt. Rodheim successfully evaded immediate capture. For 13 days, he remained at large in occupied territory, assisted by courageous local civilians and members of the French or Belgian resistance networks (or sympathetic German civilians near the border area).

**Key Details from Family Research and Contemporary Accounts:**
- Rodheim evaded Gestapo pursuit for the full 13 days.
- He was aided significantly by nuns at a monastery ("It was the Nuns who called").
- He stayed the night at the monastery.
- He came within only a few miles of reaching safety (possibly the border or a resistance safe house).
- Nazis/Gestapo arrived in the middle of the night and took him into custody.
- His fellow evader, Lt. Stefaniak, was also captured around the same period.

This evasion story highlights the extraordinary courage of both the airman and the civilians who risked their lives to assist him. Evasion in occupied Europe was extremely hazardous; many evaders were betrayed, captured quickly, or executed. Surviving 13 days was a testament to luck, training, language skills (if any), and the bravery of helpers.

## 6. Capture and Internment at Stalag Luft III

Upon capture, Rodheim was processed through Dulag Luft (transit camp) and eventually sent to Stalag Luft III at Sagan, in what was then German Silesia (now Żagan, Poland). Stalag Luft III was a Luftwaffe-run camp specifically for captured Allied aircrew officers. It is infamous for the "Great Escape" of March 1944 (just two months prior), in which 76 POWs tunneled out; 50 were murdered by the Gestapo upon recapture.

Conditions in SLIII were relatively better than many other camps (Geneva Convention protections for officers, Red Cross parcels, organized camp life with theater, sports, education), but still harsh: overcrowding, poor sanitation at times, psychological strain, and the constant threat of transfer or worse.

Rodheim would have been assigned to one of the compounds (North, East, Center, West, or South). Many American officers were in the North or West Compounds.

POW rosters and logs (e.g., those transcribed by Arnold Wright from Ewell McCright's records) exist and are being cross-referenced in ongoing research. No specific compound or detailed camp diary entry for Rodheim has surfaced in public searches yet, but family efforts continue.

Daily life involved roll calls (Appell), limited food, Red Cross food parcels (critical for survival), letter writing (censored), and camp activities to maintain morale and discipline.

## 7. The Sagan Death March and Liberation

As the Eastern Front collapsed in January 1945, with Soviet forces advancing rapidly toward Sagan, the Germans evacuated Stalag Luft III on or around 27 January 1945. Thousands of POWs were forced to march westward in brutal winter conditions – deep snow, sub-zero temperatures, inadequate clothing, and minimal food.

This "Sagan Death March" (or "The Long March") saw POWs marched to other camps or holding points, with many dying from exposure, exhaustion, disease, or strafing. Survivors were eventually liberated by advancing Allied forces (British, American, or Soviet) in April-May 1945.

Lt. Rodheim survived this ordeal, a testament to his resilience. Many accounts from SLIII POWs describe the march as one of the most grueling experiences of their captivity. Family research confirms his status as a Sagan Death March survivor.

Liberation brought medical care, processing, and eventual return to the United States via ship or air. Like many returning POWs, he would have received back pay, perhaps a period of recuperation, and transitioned to civilian life.
